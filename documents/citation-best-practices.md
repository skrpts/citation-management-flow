## Citation Best Practices Guide

### Why Citations Matter

Citations serve four essential functions in academic writing:

1. **Attribution** — giving credit to the researchers whose ideas, data, or words you have used
2. **Evidence** — supporting your claims with published, peer-reviewed evidence
3. **Traceability** — allowing readers to verify your claims by following the citation trail
4. **Positioning** — locating your work within the broader scholarly conversation

Poor citation practices — whether through carelessness or intent — can constitute academic misconduct and damage your scholarly reputation.

---

### Core Principles

#### Cite What You Have Read
Only cite sources you have actually read. If you encounter a finding in Source A that originally comes from Source B, and you have not read Source B, use a secondary citation: "(Original Author, Year, as cited in Source A Author, Year)." Do not cite Source B directly, as you cannot verify the accuracy of the representation.

#### Cite Accurately
Ensure that every citation accurately represents the cited source's claims. Common accuracy errors include:
- Overstating the certainty of a finding ("Smith proved that..." when Smith reported a preliminary association)
- Generalising beyond the source's scope (citing a study of university students as evidence about "people in general")
- Cherry-picking: citing only the results that support your argument while ignoring contradictory findings from the same source

#### Cite Sufficiently
Each substantive claim in your manuscript should be supported by at least one citation. Key claims — those central to your argument — should typically be supported by multiple sources. However, well-established facts in your field (e.g., "water boils at 100 degrees Celsius at sea level") do not require citation.

#### Cite Fairly
Citation is an ethical act. Good practice includes:
- Citing diverse sources (not just sources from your own lab, institution, or country)
- Including sources that challenge or complicate your argument, not just those that support it
- Citing the original source of an idea, not just the most recent or most famous repetition of it
- Acknowledging foundational works in your field, even if they are older

### Common Citation Mistakes

#### Orphaned Citations
An in-text citation appears in the manuscript but has no corresponding entry in the bibliography. This usually results from deleting a reference during revision without removing the in-text citation. Always run a citation check before submission.

#### Ghost References
A source appears in the bibliography but is never cited in the manuscript text. This inflates your reference count without serving the reader. Remove unused references or, if they genuinely informed your thinking, cite them in the text.

#### Citation Amnesia
Failing to cite a source that directly influenced your work. This can occur innocently (you forgot you read it) or less innocently (you want your work to appear more original). Either way, it is problematic. Keep systematic notes of everything you read.

#### Citation Padding
Adding citations that you have not read to make your bibliography appear more thorough. This is detectable (reviewers will check) and constitutes academic dishonesty. Quality over quantity.

#### String Citations
Packing multiple citations into a single parenthetical without indicating which source supports which specific claim: "(Smith, 2020; Jones, 2021; Brown, 2022; Lee, 2023)." If all four sources support the same specific point, this is acceptable. If they support different aspects of a broader claim, separate the citations and attribute each to its specific sub-claim.

#### Biased Citation
Citing only sources that support your position while omitting well-known contradictory evidence. Peer reviewers will notice this and it undermines your credibility. Engage with opposing evidence directly.

### Special Citation Situations

#### Secondary Citations
Use secondary citations ("as cited in") only when the original source is genuinely inaccessible (out of print, in a language you cannot read, unpublished). If the original is accessible, read it and cite it directly.

#### Personal Communications
Emails, conversations, and unpublished correspondence are cited in-text but typically not included in the bibliography (APA, Chicago) because readers cannot retrieve them. Always obtain permission before citing a personal communication.

#### Preprints
Preprints have not been peer-reviewed. You may cite them, but acknowledge their pre-peer-review status. If a preprint is subsequently published, update your citation to the published version.

#### Retracted Articles
Check whether any of your cited sources have been retracted. Citing retracted work without acknowledging the retraction is a serious error. If you must cite a retracted article (e.g., to discuss the retraction itself), note the retraction explicitly.

#### AI-Generated Content
If you used an AI tool in your research, follow your target journal's policy on AI citation. APA 7th edition treats AI outputs as non-recoverable sources (similar to personal communications). Some journals require disclosure of AI use in the methods section rather than via citation.

### Self-Citation Guidelines

Self-citation is legitimate when citing genuinely relevant prior work. It becomes problematic when:
- It artificially inflates your citation metrics
- It replaces citations to other researchers' more relevant work
- The self-cited work is tangentially related at best

A self-citation rate above 15-20% may raise concerns during peer review. Review your self-citations critically: for each one, ask whether a reader unfamiliar with your prior work would find the citation relevant and useful.

### Pre-Submission Citation Checklist

- [ ] Every in-text citation has a matching bibliography entry
- [ ] Every bibliography entry is cited at least once in the text
- [ ] All citations are formatted consistently in the target style
- [ ] Page numbers are provided for all direct quotations
- [ ] Secondary citations ("as cited in") are used only when the original is inaccessible
- [ ] No cited sources have been retracted (check Retraction Watch)
- [ ] Citation coverage is balanced: diverse authors, methods, geographies, and perspectives
- [ ] Self-citation rate is below 15-20%
- [ ] All substantive claims have at least one supporting citation
- [ ] Opposing or complicating evidence has been cited and addressed
